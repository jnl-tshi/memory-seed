### Summary

- Drafted `docs/3_Spec/draft/decision-level-link-sidecar-refs.md`: link-sidecar refs that terminate on
  a specific decision rather than a whole entry. Spec only, nothing implemented.

### Decisions

#### D1 - The canonical ref is the pair; the section slug is an accepted alias, not a second spelling

- D: A decision ref is authored as `<entry_id>:<dN>` (e.g. `mse_wh33mc61swqx0vkv:d2`). The Memory Trace
  section slug (`mse_abc#decisions/d2-some-slug`) is accepted on read and normalised to the pair, but is
  never canonical and never emitted by writers. Bare entry ids keep their current meaning untouched.
- R: The identity was already ratified 2026-07-20 as the pair `(entry_id, dN)`, so only the authored
  surface form was open. The decisive argument against the slug is one the ADR does not make: the slug
  **cannot address d1 of a singular `### Decision` entry**, because no `#decisions/d1-...` chunk is
  generated for that shape - 346 of ~610 entries. A form that cannot express the majority case cannot be
  canonical. Accepting it as an alias is a deliberate exception to one-spelling-per-identity, taken
  because the alternative is rejecting a paste from our own UI; the normalisation is lossless and
  `link audit --apply` emits canonical form, so aliases do not accumulate.
- A: The colon was chosen over `#` because `#` opens a YAML comment the moment anyone puts a space before
  it, whereas a mapping needs `: ` or a trailing colon - so `- mse_abc:d2` is unambiguously a scalar.
  This draft also has to close a defect found while answering JNL's question: a `#decisions/`-suffixed ref
  written today is SILENTLY TRUNCATED to the entry id, because `#` falls outside `_TRAILER_ENTRY_ID_RE`'s
  character class and the surviving prefix resolves against known entries, so no `dangling-*` issue
  fires. The sidecar looks like it recorded a decision edge and recorded an entry edge instead - neither
  honoured nor rejected. Hence `malformed-decision-ref` as an error kind with an explicit "never scrape
  the prefix and continue" rule.

#### D2 - Decision edges are a distinct edge set, never projected up to entry level

- D: A decision edge is not unioned into the entry-level `supersedes`/`evolves` lists. Consumers that do
  not model decisions ignore them entirely, exactly as they already ignore `classify_pending` stubs and
  `edge_status: not_applicable`.
- R: "D2 of B supersedes D1 of A" does not license "B supersedes A" - that overstatement is the whole
  reason the feature exists, so projecting decision edges upward at the read layer would reintroduce at
  the reader precisely what the format was designed to stop the author doing. The compatibility story is
  better this way too: existing consumers see exactly the edge set they see today, so nothing regresses
  and nothing inflates. The alternative - project up for compatibility - buys visibility in old surfaces
  at the cost of making the graph assert things no one wrote.
- A: This is the load-bearing rule of the draft; if it is ever relaxed the feature becomes a worse version
  of what already exists.

#### D3 - Intra-entry edges are legal, and the ordinal breaks the timestamp tie

- D: `mse_x:d3 evolves mse_x:d1` is legal. Decisions carry no timestamp of their own, so a decision
  endpoint resolves its entry's heading timestamp (leaving the forward-only guard unchanged in logic) -
  which means both ends of an intra-entry edge share one timestamp. Within an entry the ordinal decides:
  an intra-entry edge is legal iff the target ordinal is strictly lower than the source's.
- R: Without an explicit rule the existing guard ("legal iff A predates B") would reject a genuine "I
  changed my mind later in this session" edge with a misleading not-strictly-older message. The ordinal is
  a sound clock at that scale because decisions are written in order and entries are append-only - the
  same append-only guarantee the ADR leans on to delete its source-text fingerprint.
- A: Considered forbidding intra-entry edges outright as the simpler rule; rejected because the case is
  real and the tie-break is one comparison. Source-side decisions (`source_decision` on the block, keying
  it as `(entry_id, source_decision)`) are specified but staged second, so the smaller half of the parser
  is not designed against a shape the first half already froze.

### Summary

- Corrected the intra-entry rule in `docs/3_Spec/draft/decision-level-link-sidecar-refs.md` and made
  mixed-granularity endpoints explicit. Draft only; still nothing implemented.

### Decisions

#### D1 - Withdraw the ordinal-as-clock reasoning; decision edges must cross entries

- D: Intra-entry lifecycle edges are forbidden - both endpoints must belong to different entries, at any
  granularity, reported as `intra-entry-decision-ref`. This withdraws [[mse_j55kt6mq230zj2p4]]'s D3,
  which allowed `mse_x:d3 evolves mse_x:d1` and broke the resulting timestamp tie with the ordinal.
- R: JNL challenged the stated justification - "within an entry, lower ordinal is older, sound because
  entries are append-only" - and it does not hold. It conflates two guarantees: append-only makes ordinals
  STABLE (D2 is never renumbered because the entry is never reopened) but says nothing about them being
  CHRONOLOGICAL. An entry is authored as one act at the end of a unit of work, and D1..DN is the order the
  author chose to PRESENT the calls - by importance, by dependency, by narrative. I checked rather than
  argued: nothing in `session_logging` ties decision numbering to time, and Constitution Invariant #2's
  append-only concerns history not being rewritten, which is orthogonal. So the rule rested on a
  convention, and an integrity guard cannot: every author who numbered by importance would have a
  legitimate edge rejected with a misleading message.
  With the tie-break gone the guard has nothing to check on an intra-entry edge - one timestamp on both
  ends, no substitute clock - and the relationship is thin anyway, since decisions of one entry are
  contemporaneous by construction and entry prose already carries a same-sitting reversal. Forbidding is
  also the reversible direction: allowing later is additive, forbidding later would break authored data.
- A: The withdrawn reasoning is documented in the draft rather than deleted, because the ordinal IS a
  sound key (ADR identity) and IS a sound sort order (Trail rendering) - it fails specifically as a CLOCK,
  and that distinction is easy to lose on a re-read. Placing the prohibition inside the forward-only guard
  section rather than beside the other validation kinds is deliberate: cross-entry endpoints are what
  guarantee the guard always sees two distinct entry timestamps, so the rule belongs where the property it
  protects is stated.
  Worth recording as process: the wrong rationale read exactly like the ones that were grounded, which is
  what made it dangerous in a document someone would implement from. It was caught by a reader asking what
  the claim was based on, not by any check I ran.

#### D2 - Granularity is per-endpoint, so all four combinations are legal

- D: An entry may supersede/evolve a decision and a decision may supersede/evolve an entry; the two ends
  are independent. All four combinations (entry->entry, entry->decision, decision->entry,
  decision->decision) are legal.
- R: JNL's call, and it costs nothing: `source_decision` is already optional on the block and the decision
  suffix already optional on each ref, so the four rows are just the presence/absence matrix of the
  existing grammar - no per-combination rule is needed. The mixed rows are common in practice: a
  single-decision entry wholly retired by one call in a later entry is `decision -> entry`, and a broad
  architectural entry retiring one specific earlier call is `entry -> decision`. Requiring matched
  granularity would push both back into the overstate-or-understate choice the draft exists to remove.
- A: The mixed rows are also where the distinct-edge-set rule is easiest to get wrong, so the draft now
  names both explicitly: `decision -> entry` has a whole entry as its TARGET and so looks projectable up
  to an entry-level edge, and `entry -> decision` has a whole entry as its SOURCE and so its block looks
  ordinary. Neither projects - "D3 of B supersedes A" is not "B supersedes A".
