### Summary

- Decision diagrams are clickable in the React client (badge or reader figure -> zoom/pan modal),
  `esr` reports diagram coverage, and the three sidecars owed for 2026-07-21 are written.

### Decisions

#### D1 - Report diagram coverage as a count, never as a verdict

- D: `esr` gains a "Decision diagrams" section: how many of the session's entries carry a sidecar,
  and the date of the last sidecar anywhere. No opinion on whether any given entry needed one.
- R: JNL noticed a full day with no diagrams. Measuring showed it was not one bad day: 12 diagrams on
  2026-07-16 (the day the feature was built), then **zero across five days and 131 entries**, with the
  explain-the-skip half peaking at 5-of-20 on 07-15 and fading too. So the convention never became
  load-bearing, and nothing anywhere reported that.
  I first built the obvious thing - a keyword heuristic over the skill's own positive-trigger list -
  and measured it before shipping: it flagged **35% of all entries**, matching "worktree" in a passing
  validation line and "topology" in a feature name. A check that fires on one entry in three teaches
  its reader to skip it. It was dropped.
- A: The deciding argument was not the measurement but this module's own docstring - "Judgment stays
  with the agent - this reports, it never fixes." A trigger heuristic would have `esr` making the
  authoring judgement, which is the one thing it promises not to do. The bare fact is enough: the
  lapse is only visible as a RUN of empty days, which no single day's view shows, and that is exactly
  what "last sidecar anywhere" surfaces.

#### D2 - Clickable diagrams in React, reusing the vanilla class names

- D: A diamond badge in the Trail, or a diagram figure in the reader, opens a modal viewer with
  zoom/pan, Fit, Escape-to-close and backdrop-to-close. Zoom maths lives in `diagramZoom.ts` as pure
  functions; the class names and geometry are lifted verbatim from the vanilla stylesheet.
- R: JNL asked for parity with the vanilla JS. The React badge was an inert `<span>` whose tooltip
  said "open the entry to view" - and opening the entry only rendered the diagram inline in the
  inspector column, which is narrower than most authored diagrams. So a wide diagram was effectively
  unreadable in the React client with no way to enlarge it. Reusing vanilla's class names rather than
  inventing new ones means one stylesheet change tracks both UIs instead of them drifting.
- A: The badge fetches the entry's own chunk rather than requiring selection first, because the point
  of a preview badge is to look WITHOUT navigating away from what you are reading; it reuses the
  loaded chunk when it is already the selected entry. Zoom is extracted to a pure module for the same
  reason as graphLayout/graphEdges: the arithmetic (clamping, fit-scale, zoom-about-cursor) is where
  the bugs are and none of it needs a DOM.

#### D3 - Diagnosed the wrong cause, and was corrected by the user mid-turn

- D: The viewer's bad first render was a MISSING CSS RULE - `.diagram-stage .diagram-svg { width:
  100%; height: auto; }`, which I dropped when porting the vanilla styles. Restored.
- R: Seeing the modal render nodes strung out in a wide unreadable row, I concluded the diagrams
  themselves were at fault: `arc2d` has genuinely zero `subgraph` support (verified), and my sidecars
  used the tier-subgraph layout the `compact_mermaid_diagrams` skill prescribes, so "the house style
  is unsupported by the renderer" was a tidy story. JNL said plainly that the inspector renders the
  diagrams correctly - the same diagrams, the same renderer - which falsified it in one line. The
  difference was never the source; it was that the inspector's inline `.diagram-svg` sizes with
  `max-width` plus `overflow-x: auto`, while the viewer's stage needs `width: 100%` because the stage
  is what scales.
- A: I had already rewritten the three diagrams before the correction. They are kept, because the
  subgraph finding IS true and independently worth acting on - `arc2d` cannot draw the construct the
  skill tells authors to use, so a subgraph diagram silently loses its grouping in both UIs. That is a
  live contradiction between `compact_mermaid_diagrams.md` and the renderer, recorded here and NOT
  fixed in this entry.
  The process lesson is the recurring one for the day: a plausible story built from one observation,
  corrected by someone who checked a second surface. The cheap test I skipped was comparing the
  inspector and the viewer on the same diagram before theorising.

### Decisions

#### D1 - Decision edges are a separate stream, computed after row expansion and outside the visibility filter

- D: Build decision edges in `_decision_edges_for_rows`, called only from the `include_decisions` branch after `_expand_decision_rows`, and append them to `visible_edges` after the `limited_ids` filter rather than through it.
- R: Two independent reasons converged on the same placement. The ratified draft forbids projecting a decision edge into entry-level `supersedes`/`evolves` - "D2 of B supersedes D1 of A" does not license "B supersedes A" - so it cannot go through `build_related_entry_graph` or the sidecar union. And `limited_ids` is keyed on entry ids, so a decision row id (`{entry_id}#decisions/dN-<slug>`) is never a member: routing the edge through that filter would have dropped every one of them silently, which is the same failure mode as the parser bug this workstream exists to close, one layer up.
- A: Considered tagging each edge with a `decision: true` flag so the payload distinguishes it. Rejected: `GraphEdge` is a pydantic response model, so the field would have dragged in the v1 contract, `openapi.v1.json` and `types.ts`, and the endpoint ids already carry the distinction. No frontend change was needed at all - `trailModel.ts` keys rows by `node.id`, so a decision-row endpoint resolved on its own.
- F: `memory-trace/memory_trace/service.py`, `memory-trace/tests/test_trail_decision_edges.py`.
- T: 6 new tests. 3 fail against the pre-change `service.py` (verified by swapping in the HEAD copy and re-running); the other 3 are no-regression guards that should pass either way, the strongest being a set equality of the non-decision edge payload against a control corpus with the sidecar deleted.

#### D2 - The focus view expands over a decision edge's two entries, as membership only

- D: When an entry is focused, feed `_neighborhood` an extra entry-to-entry pair per decision edge so the far entry is displayed. The drawn edge still terminates on the decision row and nothing is added to entry-level edge lists.
- R: Found by measuring rather than reasoning: after upgrading the real ref from `mse_2m56qnk1473vmjz1` to `:d2`, focusing the source entry made the target vanish entirely, because the neighbourhood is computed from the entry-level edge set that no longer contained it. Upgrading a ref to a more precise statement must not cost visibility, or authors will decline to upgrade. Membership answers what to render; it is not an assertion that B evolves A.
- F: `memory-trace/memory_trace/service.py`.
- T: `test_focused_view_still_reaches_the_far_end_of_a_decision_edge`, which fails against the old code.

#### D3 - A dangling ordinal on an expanded entry draws nothing, rather than falling back to the entry row

- D: Fall back to the target's entry row only when that entry genuinely has no decision rows (the singular-`### Decision` shape, where the draft says the entry-level and `d1` forms denote the same edge). If the entry is expanded and the ordinal is not among its rows, drop the edge.
- R: The first cut used `decision_row.get(...) or entry_row.get(...)`, which quietly widened a bad `:d7` into an entry-level edge - reintroducing the exact overstatement the feature removes, in the one case where the author had already made a mistake. `links check` errors on `dangling-decision-ref` so this cannot reach a clean corpus, but the renderer should not depend on the validator having run.
- F: `memory-trace/memory_trace/service.py`, `memory-trace/tests/test_trail_decision_edges.py`.

#### D4 - The ADR corpus table is re-derived from a committed script, and disagrees with both prior counts

- R: The ADR's 2026-07-20 hand count (612 entries) and the 2026-07-21 recount (580) were both unreproducible, and the honest finding is that the new numbers match neither. The mechanism turned out to be two entry splitters over byte-identical files: `_ENTRY_HEADING_RE` requires a `HH:MM` stamp and yields 564, while the semantic-cache entry extractor accepts the date-only `## YYYY-MM-DD - title` headings the May-2026 entries use and yields 589 - which is exactly the `entry_count` Memory Trace displays, so that figure has been on screen the whole time. 580 reproduces under neither splitter, on this branch or on the primary checkout. - A: The 521/115 figures do reproduce exactly, which is the corroboration that matters - they come from the shipped `_entry_decision_ordinals`, so coverage claims and validation cannot drift apart.
- F: `scripts/count_decision_shapes.py`, `docs/3_Spec/draft/adr-lifecycle-sidecar-contract.md`, `docs/3_Spec/draft/decision-level-link-sidecar-refs.md`.
